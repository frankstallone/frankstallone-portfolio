Anger, Sloth Prince Hamlet: Although many believe Hamlet to be severely disturbed, we believe that Hamlet is quite the contrary. He is a boy at heart, with the mind of a philosopher. Young Hamlet lacks the gut necessary to carry out all the things he talks about doing. One thing that the entire play deals with is Hamlet's inability to respect himself in his inability to carry out what he truly wants to do because he feels morally it is wrong. Could it be that Hamlet is too morally correct in his inability to avenge his father's murder? Or could it be that Hamlet acts as anyone of us would act now. Hamlet is not a character to be questioned, but a reality that does not require it. We are Hamlet, and it is possible that this is truly what Shakespeare wanted us to realize.

Lust: The Queen's Motivation: Men, brother in law, son, and sex? What is up with this lady? Her husband is so forgottenly murdered that she marries his own brother. She loves her son and lives for him but in some people's eyes she feels things for him (you know, raw, down and dirty feelings!). Truly Gertrude is a nauseating character while she lives up to Hamlets ideas about women. She is just like naive Ophelia in that but this woman is severely disturbed, and in that it is she that should have her sanity questioned.

Envy, Lust Claudius or shall we call him "funkle"? This man, is a man completely opposite in morals than those followed by Hamlet. He is in, shall we say, a completely different universe in comparison to the morals of Hamlet. As Hamlet is completely indecisive and unable to follow through with the majority of his ideas, his "funkle" is a man, able to murder his own brother, take his crown and throne, attempt to murder his own nephew, while living a completely incestuous relationship with his sister-in-law. He definitely takes on the role of the bad guy in Hamlet in all of these actions, while it seems as though the remorse felt by him is little in his inability to ask for God's forgiveness and pardon (he knows he would have probably done it all again). This king comes to a well-deserved end, filled with poison as lethal as cyanide, and a vacation to Hell.

Carelessness Poor sweet Ophelia, a character scarred by her own weakness and frailty. In mind and body Ophelia was soft. She allowed for Hamlet to take her virginity, she succumbed to the beckoning call of the King and Queen to betray her only Love, and she lost her sanity when her sponge-like father Polonious was killed by Hamlet. Ophelia's weakness supported greatly Hamlet's comment on women, "Frailty, thy name is woman."

Gluttony Polonius is a sponge. Driven by his own need for power, Polonius is like a sponge for words. Using his great ability as a spy (so great that he sneezed his way to hell) and as an advisor to the king he thinks he will know all and therefor have all. He, like Hamlet, respects knowledge; as they felt the mind to be the only weapon needed to win in life. But to what he uses the knowledge for shows his overwhelming flaw of greed for knowledge and power. This greed eventually means Polonius' death.

Perfect -Horatio is a good man in every aspect. He is the only character unable to be bought by the King, and the only character given full trust by Hamlet. He remains Hamlet's best friend from the beginning till the end of the play, never doubting Hamlet's mind, actions, or character. Horatio is a man who deserves the utmost respect and admiration of any reader of Shakespeare's Hamlet, for his honor, for his trust in Hamlet, and for his love for Hamlet. Horatio would have killed himself along his friend Hamlet not because he was a lapdog, but because his existence was dedicated to Hamlet and he felt empty without a friend who could trust him the way Hamlet did.

Pride Laertes is a man both honorable and noble deep down; while on the surface appears to be a gullible puppet of the King. He is responsible for both his and Hamlet's undoing, in his use of a poison dipped sword. Too overcome with grief for the losses of his sister and father, he sees only Hamlet's guilt in these deaths and looks beyond reasoning. For this he is more like Fortinbraus, another man able to act in the honor of his father while putting aside morals like those of Hamlet. Laertes only strays from goodness in these moments, but in his true honor and nobility, realizes his mistake and makes amends with the young Hamlet. Laertes is a man, rash but real, and in his hasty mistakes he holds many qualities we all show in our day to day lives.

Ghost Hamlet: A good man, king, and father who's only cause now is to suffer in the depths of Hell, while he waits for his vengeance by his son. Put through the anguish of Hell, and the deceit of his incestuous wife/sister in law.
